# Section 2: Kitchen Notes

## Voices from the 2022 Residency

*The following is synthesized from the On Track Consulting evaluation report (June 2022) and guest attendance documentation. Each voice speaks about their experience and what mattered most.*

## From the Fellows

### Vibha Galhotra (Conceptual Artist, New Delhi)

**On the disorientation week:** "The orientation included engaging tours to understand some of the layers and complexities that make Jerusalem unique. I have never experienced such a strong religious culture experience. We delved into all the political, economic, and social layers in Israel, leaving room for us to pick and choose. Other residencies have the host organization explain the local scene from their own perspective. The Jerusalem Fellowship gives me more clarity. Here I think of politics as differences, which is a big change in my work."

**On studio space:** "I worried about having studio space and then I discovered that the city of Jerusalem is my studio."

**On her work:** Vibha developed a project exploring how three Abrahamic religions (Judaism, Christianity, Islam) understand humanity's relationship to nature. She worked with religious leaders from 16 different sects, recording their verses and teachings. With filmmaker Aaron Paz, she created a sound installation made of Jerusalem stone, featuring the recorded voices. The piece was presented at Muslala's rooftop gallery and drew deep engagement from Jerusalem's cultural community. Before she left, the Tower of David's executive staff approached her about creating a larger-scale installation at their site.

### Claudia Lavista (Choreographer/Dancer, Mazatlán, Mexico)

**On arrival and collaboration:** "I arrived in a new country with no language skills. My host Elad was very busy but tried his best to help me as well. We needed the program to provide a producer for all the artists—someone who is accessible and reliable and understands all the media, contexts and language that can help us put together the idea we want to work with."

**On navigation:** "Jerusalem is a labyrinth. You need to be strong and find your way. I suggest having someone to talk to every so often. These are very strong experiences, and we are not in our comfort zone with so many overwhelming details."

**On discovery:** "The perception out there is that Israeli dance is very European. But I discovered this not to be true. There is a desert-based wildness here. A beautiful simplicity that I didn't know existed. Even the fancy people here are down to earth and indigenous, like I am. I find them smart, intelligent, and educated with a simple sense of humanity that I love."

**On her work and legacy:** Claudia created two dance projects. The first was a sold-out performance on May 12 at the Brown Hotel, featuring a pas de deux on the hotel bar. The second was a documentary video filmed at three sites (Ein Prat nature preserve, Lifta, and the Tower of David), creating footage that will be incorporated into c.a.t.a.m.o.n's festival in fall 2022. She taught master classes at the Jerusalem Academy of Music and Dance and was invited to return to teach in 2023. She arranged for Israeli students to visit her studio in Mexico and planned a reciprocal visit for her host Elad and c.a.t.a.m.o.n.

**On the experience overall:** "I allowed the process to direct me. Now I have an entire community of friends here and will be returning next year. I opened a window and found a garden. Now it will grow."

### Anna Lublina (Interdisciplinary Performance Artist, New York, via Germany)

**On the impact:** "Overall, I made real friendships and got to understand Jerusalem on a very, very deep level—which I think was the point. And was definitely achieved. This was an amazing life changing experience in many ways. I developed strong relations with people, and I will only begin to understand it once I've had time to digest it all."

**On surprise discoveries:** "It's been a true joy. I've never met this many types of Jews in my life—it is amazing. Especially among the younger people, so many are reaching back to special practices from where their families come from and integrating them into their artistic practice. I feel super connected to that. That's been a nice surprise and a new way of seeing this place."

**On her work:** Anna collaborated with the Bloomfield Science Museum to explore synchronization and connection through weaving. She engaged audiences in interactive demonstrations where participants created a "human weaving loom," embodying synchronization through their bodies. The salon was well-attended and creatively led; however, museum staff added a curator panel discussion that wasn't fully integrated into Anna's vision. She worked primarily on concept development rather than producing a finished installation.

### Sofia Borges (Visual/Conceptual Artist, São Paulo, Brazil)

**On teaching at Ibdaa:** "What we started at Ibdaa needs to continue for the sake of humanity. It was really something special that has great potential to change perspectives and enrich the children's lives through art. There was a shared sense of something important taking place."

**On student impact:** Sofia conducted workshops teaching experimental techniques for digital photography and image transformation. She took students on field trips to museums and galleries. She introduced them to local Israeli artists. She helped them understand that "art is about self-expression and giving them tools to do so." Unlike the other Fellows, Sofia established a framework for continuing the work after the residency. She connected Jerusalem artists to the school to continue teaching.

**Assessment from principal Melham Bader:** "Sofia is the best choice for us. She understood our limitations as a school with its rigid schedule and is willing to work within the framework. This is the first time that the school hosted a residency. Despite many difficulties along the way, the experience for the students was significant and valuable."

## From Host Organization Directors

### Elad Schechter (c.a.t.a.m.o.n Dance Group)

**On the partnership:** "I hoped I'd get an artist to learn from who would enrich my work. Claudia is this person. I am fed up with product-based projects and happy/excited to get someone who is involved in a process-based project. We can just learn from each other and enjoy. The demands on my time are more extreme than I anticipated. The flip side of that is that it is a fantastic and challenging project. I congratulate that kind of experience."

**On resources:** "She is used to having a studio with full time dancers on staff. c.a.t.a.m.o.n is a project-based company. The artists are chosen by project and when not working with c.a.t.a.m.o.n, they are busy with other projects. There is no pool for Claudia to readily choose from. There is a clear difference in working culture and how time is respected. It was difficult for Claudia to find people to work with at the outset."

**On time:** "It is fantastic and challenging at the same time. Ten weeks is a good period of time to form a deep, meaningful relationship and a working process."

**Future plan:** Elad anticipates reciprocal exchange to Mexico and plans to present Claudia's work at other venues. Claudia invited him and his company to Mexico.

### Melham Bader (Ibdaa School of the Arts, East Jerusalem)

**On the opportunity:** "This is a great opportunity for our students to have an international artist in the school. We are all involved in the process. This Fellowship is giving our school visibility and legitimacy as an Arab model for a school of the arts. This is an opportunity to be collaborative, bring people together to come up with new ideas and network."

**On challenges:** Ibdaa is a school, not a traditional art institution, which created expectations misalignment. Sofia needed resources (transportation to museums, materials, translation support) that the school's budget could not absorb. Language barriers required ad-hoc translation solutions. However, Sofia was willing to work within constraints.

**Assessment:** "Despite the difficulties, Sofia laid the groundwork for maintaining her work with Ibdaa. She connected many of the artists she met during her residency with the school so they may continue to work with the children and staff at the school and help increase the culture of art in the first Palestinian school for the arts."

### Maya Halevy (Bloomfield Science Museum)

**On collaboration:** "Anna challenged us to reach beyond our comfort zone. As an outsider with fresh ideas, helping her was a challenge. We thought that Anna would be able to fit into the ongoing work of the staff, and to work more with museum visitors which was not the case. Anna arrived with two concepts in mind, which she developed with a creative group she met on site."

**On expectations:** "As a first-time host of a residency, it benefitted by learning what works and what doesn't. Due to Anna's age and relative inexperience, it was felt that she probably needed more mentoring and handholding than we were able to give at that time."

**On outcomes:** "There was some disappointment expressed as the host felt that they were the only project of the Fellowship where nothing tangible of value was left behind. If it's a work in progress, what is the continuation of that progress when the residency ends?"

**Assessment:** "Overall, the residency was a success. The fellowship introduced them to new fields that are interactive with all forms of the arts. They are looking forward to hosting another residency."

### Matan Israeli (Muslala)

**On process vs. outcome:** "I want to learn from Vibha's art practice. Although Matan was less involved than he would have preferred, he felt the residency program was impressive with lots of exposure to information and people. As CEO of Muslala, he just didn't have the bandwidth to work intensively with Vibha."

**On resources:** "Vibha needed a producer from the start. The funding was insufficient for what Vibha wanted to do. She was able to complete her project (in record time), with a producer that Elana recruited and contingency funds from the fellowship."

**On success:** "In Matan's eyes, there is no objective measurement of success. Success is in the interaction between the people, and in that it was very successful. We have to limit the aspirations of the artist for the project."

## From External Stakeholders

### Ruth Cummings (Jerusalem Culture Unlimited, East Jerusalem Advisor)

"The Jerusalem Fellows program was a shot of B12 for every person who participated—artist, host, guests, viewers. It was a doppler effect of creativity radiating out into the city."

### Diana Lipton (Cultural Scholar, Informal Curator)

Diana was "adopted" by the Fellows and vice versa. She invited them to her home multiple times and attended most events. "Jerusalem was enriched by the artists and many of them extended their interactions far beyond their host organizations. Vibha is a master at finding what she wants and collected interesting characters across the city."

### Naomi Schachter (National Library of Israel)

"There was a real sense of the artists penetrating and engaging with large swaths of the Jerusalem community."
