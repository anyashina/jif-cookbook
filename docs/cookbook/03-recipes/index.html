How the Program Actually Runs

The Selection Process: Building the Nominator Network

The program does not post an open call. It works through a curated nominator network built over years.

Timeline:

  • Year 1 (nominator cultivation): Elise and advisory committee identify and cultivate 20-30 cultural leaders, curators, and presenters in relevant disciplines (visual art, performance, interdisciplinary practice, architecture, urban planning) who have demonstrated commitment to the work and credibility with artists. This relationship-building happens continuously; it does not pause between cohorts.

  • Months 4-5 before cohort (nomination): Once host organizations are confirmed, their artistic discipline needs are communicated to nominators. A formal call for nominations is issued. Nominators are expected to present artists who will engage with the specific tension the program addresses (see Section 5 on Methodology of Serendipity).

  • Months 3-4 before cohort (vetting): An independent vetting panel reviews nominations. Finalists are identified.

  • Months 2-3 before cohort (application and conversation): Finalists apply and have substantive conversations with host organization directors to assess mutual fit and sensibilities. Applications are reviewed by an independent panel. Host organizations make final selections.

2022 Example:

  • 50 artists nominated by international nominations committee
  • Approximately 25 selected by panel to apply
  • 12 artists applied to Jerusalem residency
  • 4 artists chosen by panel
  • Final selections made by host organizations

Selection Criteria: The program seeks artists with demonstrated expertise in their field AND demonstrated capacity to engage with other people as part of their creative practice. From 2023 Bloomberg proposal: “Criteria include artists’ demonstrated ability to engage with difficult social and political questions through creative practice. The program is not looking for artists making work about the Palestinian-Israeli conflict in the abstract; it’s looking for artists who can work at the specific tensions that host organizations are navigating.”

Why This Matters: A 6-month nomination and selection timeline allows the program to be intentional about who enters the room. It also requires that the nominator network is already warm and established before any formal recruitment begins. This is why the program cannot be launched quickly.

Host Organization Vetting and Commitment

The program’s success depends entirely on host organizations serving as bridges, not gatekeepers.

What Host Organizations Do:

  • Provide physical and intellectual space for the Fellow to develop a collaborative project
  • Facilitate introductions to community members, artists, and cultural participants who can engage with the Fellow’s work
  • Help the Fellow navigate the city, the politics, and the social structures
  • Participate in weekly salons coordinated by the JIF team
  • Receive a stipend to cover additional staffing, space rental, and operational support

Host Organization Selection Timeline: 18-24 months minimum from initial relationship-building to formal commitment. This timeline accounts for:

  • 6-12 months: Ongoing relationship-building, understanding the organization’s capacity and politics
  • 6-12 months: Formal conversations about commitment, resource requirements, and exit clauses
  • Final 3 months: Legal/financial agreements

The 2022 program benefited from this timeline: some host organizations had been in conversation with Elise for 12+ years through the prior program.

Host Organization Geography: A mix of East and West Jerusalem organizations is essential to the model. The 2023 proposal notes specific challenges in East Jerusalem: organizations operating under “non-normalization” frameworks (refusing Israeli funding and refusing to work with Israelis) require different support and communication strategies. This is why the 2027 plan includes formal collaboration with Ruth Cummings and Jerusalem Culture Unlimited, whose staff and credibility give JIF entrée into East Jerusalem communities.

2022 Host Organizations (confirmed with director feedback):

  • c.a.t.a.m.o.n Dance Group (Elad Schechter, CEO, West Jerusalem, performance-focused)
  • Ibdaa School of the Arts (Melham Bader, Principal, Sheikh Jarrah, East Jerusalem, education-focused)
  • Bloomfield Science Museum (Maya Halevy, Director, West Jerusalem, science/public engagement)
  • Muslala (Matan Israeli, Director, Clal Building, mixed East/West, community arts, food, ecology)

2027 Expressed Interest (not yet committed): Bezalel, Museum on the Seam, Sinsila, Ibdaa School of the Arts (continuation), Jerusalem Culture Unlimited, Raed Said (musician), Jerusalem Academy of Music and Dance, Israel Story.

Housing and Living Situation

Fellows live in shared apartments in intentional locations that position them at or near the East-West divide. This is not accident; it’s structural. The disorientation and bridge-building work requires that Fellows wake up in contested territory.

Housing Logistics:

  • Shared apartments (typically 2 Fellows per unit)
  • Located in neighborhoods that cross or border the seam
  • Fully subsidized, including utilities
  • 12 weeks duration (program runs 10 weeks plus pre-arrival and post-residency time)

2022 Actuals: Location was Musrara, a mixed neighborhood. Four Fellows shared one apartment initially, which created privacy and group dynamic challenges per evaluation feedback. Future recommendation: two apartments with two Fellows each, personality-matched through surveys.

Evaluation Feedback: “The apartment was well located and beautiful. However, the four artists did not have enough personal space. It is felt that even a larger home would not be optimal. Rather two people sharing separate apartments is more appropriate and the personalities should be matched to the fullest extent possible.”

The Disorientation Week

The first seven days are deliberately structured around exposure to competing narratives.

Week 1 Structure:

  • Daily guided tours led by professional guides from both sides of Jerusalem
  • Entry into different religious and cultural sites with context-setting conversations
  • Introduction to different versions of Jerusalem’s history, current politics, and social structure
  • Meetings with cultural leaders from East and West
  • Intentionally NO introduction to Fellow’s assigned host organization (that begins Week 2)

The Deliberate Disorientation: The goal is not clarity. It’s productive confusion. Fellows arrive oriented to their own practice and perspective. They leave Week 1 knowing they have entered a place where there are multiple simultaneous truths, multiple communities, multiple claims, and no simple answer. This is the necessary foundation for the collaborative work that follows.

2022 Experience: Evaluation noted: “The schedule was comprehensive, balanced and well organized. Nonetheless, the days were long, and the visitors tired easily, and it was the coldest Jerusalem in 100 years. Walking through the city was an exhausting task for them. English is a second language for some, and they found it difficult to absorb the long tour guide explanations while walking through the city.”

Recommendations for Future: Schedule disorientation week to avoid major holidays (2022 coincided with Passover, Easter, Ramadan, creating vacation closures and scheduling confusion). Build in rest time and allow first day unscheduled. Provide written materials and sit-down sessions, not just walking tours. Include trained facilitator for religious/political discussions.

Production Notes: Week 1 requires 2-3 professional guides with cultural credibility, arranged transportation, meals, and entry fees. The 2026-2027 budget allocates approximately $1,400-$1,500 for this week.

Weekly Salons

Salons are the connective tissue of the program. They are hosted three times per week for ten weeks (approximately 36 events), each drawing 15-25 invited guests.

Salon Structure:

  • Evening gathering (typically 6-9pm)
  • Dinner or substantial refreshments provided
  • Guest list intentionally curated by communications manager (invitations do not go public)
  • Conversation often prompted by the Fellow’s work-in-progress or a topic of mutual interest
  • Language: typically bilingual (Hebrew and English minimum; Arabic when relevant to guests and topic)

Who Attends:

  • Artists and cultural practitioners
  • Host organization members and their networks
  • Academic and intellectual community members
  • Community members and activists nominated by host organizations
  • Municipal officials and cultural leaders (selective)

What Salons Are Not: They are not lectures. They are not panels. They are not performances (though artistic elements sometimes appear). They are intimate gatherings where 15-25 people have sustained conversation about difficult topics, usually prompted by art-making.

Labor Intensity: One salon per week is approximately 8-12 hours of work for the communications manager (planning, invitation curation, translation preparation, setup, facilitation, cleanup). Three per week means the communications manager is doing 24-36 hours of weekly salon work alone. This is why the role cannot be part-time or absorbed into other responsibilities.

2022 Examples:

Vibha’s Salon (Muslala rooftop): Vibha worked with Jerusalem Food Rescuers to create a dinner installation. Food was arranged on the table corresponding to Israel’s waters and landscape. She explored water and agriculture through the lens of three Abrahamic religions’ teachings on nature. Subsequent to the salon, Food Rescuers became the caterers for the Fellowship.

Claudia’s Salon (c.a.t.a.m.o.n): Small, intimate gathering of approximately 30 dancers and choreographers. Claudia presented the history of her Mexican dance company and school and her interest in building an international network. Every participant was included in the activity, leading to ongoing discussions throughout the evening and ongoing collaborations.

Anna’s Salon (Bloomfield Science Museum): Organized with museum staff and well-attended. Anna engaged audiences in her work on synchronicity through weaving by connecting several people in a demonstration. Evening opened with general explanation, followed by buffet dinner created by Jerusalem Food Rescuers. Museum CEO added a curator panel discussion after dinner. Some disconnect noted between the two program sections; future salons should integrate all elements more fully.

Sofia’s Salon (Ibdaa School, East Jerusalem): Attended predominantly by parents, students, and educators. Created exceptional experience for East Jerusalem community exposure. West Jerusalem curators, artists, and press traveled to East Jerusalem to attend.

Evaluation Feedback:

  • “Overscheduling: The artists felt that while they wanted to be supportive of their fellow artists, this created an overload of salons and events to attend.”
  • “The artist should present themselves and their work at the salon. Some disappointment was expressed that there wasn’t enough opportunity to ‘get to know’ the artist at some of the salons.”
  • “The timings of the salons were problematic. Some artists only had a couple of days between their salon and work in progress, while others were very early in the residency. Perhaps all the salons should be scheduled for week 3 of the residency.”
  • “The evenings needed to be more tightly scheduled; there was too much waiting around for things to start. The artists should be given more specific guidelines regarding how long they should speak.”

Fellow Collaboration with Host Organization

Each Fellow works with an assigned host organization on a project of mutual interest. The project develops over the course of the residency through conversations between the Fellow and the host organization director.

Project Development Timeline:

  • Pre-residency (3-6 months): Fellow and host organization director have video conversations to establish mutual goals and artistic sensibilities
  • Week 1: Fellow meets host organization in person; disorientation week proceeds in parallel
  • Weeks 2-8: Weekly collaboration, with the Fellow working in the host organization’s space when possible
  • Week 9: Work-in-progress presentation (internal to program participants)
  • Week 10: Project may continue or may have concluded; this week is open for processing

Project Examples (2022):

  • Vibha Galhotra worked with Muslala to develop sound installation exploring human-nature relationships across three Abrahamic traditions
  • Sofia Borges established a residency program framework at Ibdaa School for the Arts in Sheikh Jarrah (ongoing beyond the Fellowship)
  • Claudia Lavista created performance projects at c.a.t.a.m.o.n and a documentary video at multiple Jerusalem locations
  • Anna Lublina explored synchronization through weaving with Bloomfield Science Museum

What Makes This Work: Host organizations are not gatekeepers or taskmasters. They are collaborators who genuinely want to work with the artist and genuinely have resources and access that the artist needs to make work. If host organizations see the Fellowship as a burden or a box they must tick, the program collapses.

Evaluation Finding: “Each artist spent time at a specific location in Jerusalem, where they showed and displayed their work. Throughout their stay in Jerusalem, the four artists have been living together in an apartment in Musrara and have received an in-depth introduction to Jerusalem’s cultural landscape while building their specific projects and touring the city. On their own they met many remarkable Jerusalemites who connected them with people and parts of the city that JIF staff could not have reached directly.”

Documentation and Evaluation

The program includes embedded evaluation and documentation.

Documentation:

  • Video and photo documentation throughout
  • Salon conversations recorded (with consent; see permissions section)
  • Selected archival materials (emails, planning documents, photos) collected for program records

Evaluation:

  • Independent evaluator engaged at beginning of residency
  • Regular check-ins with Fellows, host organizations, and participants
  • Final interview with evaluator during Fellows’ last week
  • Written evaluation report delivered within 6-8 weeks of program end

2022 Evaluation: On Track Consulting evaluation (June 2022, 52 pages) assessed program impact, operational effectiveness, and replicability. Key findings flagged the program as successful despite compressed timelines and recommended specific tweaks for future cycles.

(iii) a facilitator to meet with them once every two weeks to help them process their experiences.”

Contingency and Crisis Planning

The 2022 program ran during a relatively stable political moment (though no moment in Jerusalem is truly stable). Planning documents note that contingency reserves of 10% are necessary.

Identified Risks:

  • Political escalation (October 7, 2023 resulted in program suspension)
  • Weather events (2022 notes: “coldest March on record” created unexpected logistics challenges)
  • Health incidents among Fellows or staff
  • Housing disruption (security situation, availability)

How to Plan for This:

  • Maintain contingency reserve of 10% of annual budget
  • Build in 3-week flexibility in program dates (if October start is unsafe, program can delay to mid-October or push to January)
  • Establish clear communication protocols with host organizations about pause/resume decisions
  • Have alternative housing options identified in advance
  • Maintain relationships with medical providers and crisis counselors

2022 Example: Contingency funds were used when Claudia needed insurance to film at Ein Prat nature preserve, enabling that project’s completion.
