How the Program Actually Runs

The Selection Process: Building the Nominator Network

The program does not post an open call. It works through a curated nominator network built over years.

Timeline:

Year 1 (nominator cultivation): Elise and advisory committee identify and cultivate 20-30 cultural leaders, curators, and presenters in relevant disciplines (visual art, performance, interdisciplinary practice, architecture, urban planning) who have demonstrated commitment to the work and credibility with artists. This relationship-building happens continuously; it does not pause between cohorts.

Months 4-5 before cohort (nomination): Once host organizations are confirmed, their artistic discipline needs are communicated to nominators. A formal call for nominations is issued. Nominators are expected to present artists who will engage with the specific tension the program addresses (see Section 5 on Methodology of Serendipity).

Months 3-4 before cohort (vetting): An independent vetting panel reviews nominations. Finalists are identified.

Months 2-3 before cohort (application and conversation): Finalists apply and have substantive conversations with host organization directors to assess mutual fit and sensibilities. Applications are reviewed by an independent panel. Host organizations make final selections.

2022 Example:

  • 50 artists nominated by international nominations committee
  • Approximately 25 selected by panel to apply
  • 12 artists applied to Jerusalem residency
  • 4 artists chosen by panel
  • Final selections made by host organizations

Selection Criteria:

The program seeks artists with demonstrated expertise in their field AND demonstrated capacity to engage with other people as part of their creative practice. From 2023 Bloomberg proposal: “Criteria include artists’ demonstrated ability to engage with difficult social and political questions through creative practice. The program is not looking for artists making work about the Palestinian-Israeli conflict in the abstract; it’s looking for artists who can work at the specific tensions that host organizations are navigating.”

Why This Matters:

A 6-month nomination and selection timeline allows the program to be intentional about who enters the room. It also requires that the nominator network is already warm and established before any formal recruitment begins. This is why the program cannot be launched quickly.

Host Organization Vetting and Commitment

The program’s success depends entirely on host organizations serving as bridges, not gatekeepers.

What Host Organizations Do:

  • Provide physical and intellectual space for the Fellow to develop a collaborative project
  • Facilitate introductions to community members, artists, and cultural participants who can engage with the Fellow’s work
  • Help the Fellow navigate the city, the politics, and the social structures
  • Participate in weekly salons coordinated by the JIF team
  • Receive a stipend to cover additional staffing, space rental, and operational support

Host Organization Selection Timeline:

18-24 months minimum from initial relationship-building to formal commitment. This timeline accounts for:

  • 6-12 months: Ongoing relationship-building, understanding the organization’s capacity and politics
  • 6-12 months: Formal conversations about commitment, resource requirements, and exit clauses
  • Final 3 months: Legal/financial agreements

The 2022 program benefited from this timeline: some host organizations had been in conversation with Elise for 12+ years through the prior program.

Host Organization Geography:

A mix of East and West Jerusalem organizations is essential to the model. The 2023 proposal notes specific challenges in East Jerusalem: organizations operating under “non-normalization” frameworks (refusing Israeli funding and refusing to work with Israelis) require different support and communication strategies. This is why the 2027 plan includes formal collaboration with Ruth Cummings and Jerusalem Culture Unlimited, whose staff and credibility give JIF entrée into East Jerusalem communities.

2022 Host Organizations (confirmed with director feedback):

  • c.a.t.a.m.o.n Dance Group (Elad Schechter, CEO, West Jerusalem, performance-focused)
  • Ibdaa School of the Arts (Melham Bader, Principal, Sheikh Jarrah, East Jerusalem, education-focused)
  • Bloomfield Science Museum (Maya Halevy, Director, West Jerusalem, science/public engagement)
  • Muslala (Matan Israeli, Director, Clal Building, mixed East/West, community arts, food, ecology)

2027 Expressed Interest (not yet committed):

Bezalel, Museum on the Seam, Sinsila, Ibdaa School of the Arts (continuation), Jerusalem Culture Unlimited, Raed Said (musician), Jerusalem Academy of Music and Dance, Israel Story.

Housing and Living Situation

Fellows live in shared apartments in intentional locations that position them at or near the East-West divide. This is not accident; it’s structural. The disorientation and bridge-building work requires that Fellows wake up in contested territory.

Housing Logistics:

  • Shared apartments (typically 2 Fellows per unit)
  • Located in neighborhoods that cross or border the seam
  • Fully subsidized, including utilities
  • 12 weeks duration (program runs 10 weeks plus pre-arrival and post-residency time)

2022 Actuals:

Location was Musrara, a mixed neighborhood. Four Fellows shared one apartment initially, which created privacy and group dynamic challenges per evaluation feedback. Future recommendation: two apartments with two Fellows each, personality-matched through surveys.

Evaluation Feedback: “The apartment was well located and beautiful. However, the four artists did not have enough personal space. It is felt that even a larger home would not be optimal. Rather two people sharing separate apartments is more appropriate and the personalities should be matched to the fullest extent possible.”

The Disorientation Week

The first seven days are deliberately structured around exposure to competing narratives.

Week 1 Structure:

  • Daily guided tours led by professional guides from both sides of Jerusalem
  • Entry into different religious and cultural sites with context-setting conversations
  • Introduction to different versions of Jerusalem’s history, current politics, and social structure
  • Meetings with cultural leaders from East and West
  • Intentionally NO introduction to Fellow’s assigned host organization (that begins Week 2)

The Deliberate Disorientation:

The goal is not clarity. It’s productive confusion. Fellows arrive oriented to their own practice and perspective. They leave Week 1 knowing they have entered a place where there are multiple simultaneous truths, multiple communities, multiple claims, and no simple answer. This is the necessary foundation for the collaborative work that follows.

2022 Experience:

Evaluation noted: “The schedule was comprehensive, balanced and well organized. Nonetheless, the days were long, and the visitors tired easily, and it was the coldest Jerusalem in 100 years. Walking through the city was an exhausting task for them. English is a second language for some, and they found it difficult to absorb the long tour guide explanations while walking through the city.”

Recommendations for Future:

Schedule disorientation week to avoid major holidays (2022 coincided with Passover, Easter, Ramadan, creating vacation closures and scheduling confusion). Build in rest time and allow first day unscheduled. Provide written materials and sit-down sessions, not just walking tours. Include trained facilitator for religious/political discussions.

Production Notes:

Week 1 requires 2-3 professional guides with cultural credibility, arranged transportation, meals, and entry fees. The 2026-2027 budget allocates approximately $1,400-$1,500 for this week.

Weekly Salons

Salons are the connective tissue of the program. They are hosted three times per week for ten weeks (approximately 36 events), each drawing 15-25 invited guests.

Salon Structure:

  • Evening gathering (typically 6-9pm)
  • Dinner or substantial refreshments provided
  • Guest list intentionally curated by communications manager (invitations do not go public)
  • Conversation often prompted by the Fellow’s work-in-progress or a topic of mutual interest
  • Language: typically bilingual (Hebrew and English minimum; Arabic when relevant to guests and topic)

Who Attends:

  • Artists and cultural practitioners
  • Host organization members and their networks
  • Academic and intellectual community members
  • Community members and activists nominated by host organizations
  • Municipal officials and cultural leaders (selective)

What Salons Are Not:

They are not lectures. They are not panels. They are not performances (though artistic elements sometimes appear). They are intimate gatherings where 15-25 people have sustained conversation about difficult topics, usually prompted by art-making.

Labor Intensity:

One salon per week is approximately 8-12 hours of work for the communications manager (planning, invitation curation, translation preparation, setup, facilitation, cleanup). Three per week means the communications manager is doing 24-36 hours of weekly salon work alone. This is why the role cannot be part-time or absorbed into other responsibilities.

2022 Examples:

Vibha’s Salon (Muslala rooftop): Vibha worked with Jerusalem Food Rescuers to create a dinner installation. Food was arranged on the table corresponding to Israel’s waters and landscape. She explored water and agriculture through the lens of three Abrahamic religions’ teachings on nature. Subsequent to the salon, Food Rescuers became the caterers for the Fellowship.

Claudia’s Salon (c.a.t.a.m.o.n): Small, intimate gathering of approximately 30 dancers and choreographers. Claudia presented the history of her Mexican dance company and school and her interest in building an international network. Every participant was included in the activity, leading to ongoing discussions throughout the evening and ongoing collaborations.

Anna’s Salon (Bloomfield Science Museum): Organized with museum staff and well-attended. Anna engaged audiences in her work on synchronicity through weaving by connecting several people in a demonstration. Evening opened with general explanation, followed by buffet dinner created by Jerusalem Food Rescuers. Museum CEO added a curator panel discussion after dinner. Some disconnect noted between the two program sections; future salons should integrate all elements more fully.

Sofia’s Salon (Ibdaa School, East Jerusalem): Attended predominantly by parents, students, and educators. Created exceptional experience for East Jerusalem community exposure. West Jerusalem curators, artists, and press traveled to East Jerusalem to attend.

Evaluation Feedback:

  • “Overscheduling: The artists felt that while they wanted to be supportive of their fellow artists, this created an overload of salons and events to attend.”
  • “The artist should present themselves and their work at the salon. Some disappointment was expressed that there wasn’t enough opportunity to ‘get to know’ the artist at some of the salons.”
  • “The timings of the salons were problematic. Some artists only had a couple of days between their salon and work in progress, while others were very early in the residency. Perhaps all the salons should be scheduled for week 3 of the residency.”
  • “The evenings needed to be more tightly scheduled; there was too much waiting around for things to start. The artists should be given more specific guidelines regarding how long they should speak.”

Fellow Collaboration with Host Organization

Each Fellow works with an assigned host organization on a project of mutual interest. The project develops over the course of the residency through conversations between the Fellow and the host organization director.

Project Development Timeline:

  • Pre-residency (3-6 months): Fellow and host organization director have video conversations to establish mutual goals and artistic sensibilities
  • Week 1: Fellow meets host organization in person; disorientation week proceeds in parallel
  • Weeks 2-8: Weekly collaboration, with the Fellow working in the host organization’s space when possible
  • Week 9: Work-in-progress presentation (internal to program participants)
  • Week 10: Project may continue or may have concluded; this week is open for processing

Project Examples (2022):

  • Vibha Galhotra worked with Muslala to develop sound installation exploring human-nature relationships across three Abrahamic traditions
  • Sofia Borges established a residency program framework at Ibdaa School for the Arts in Sheikh Jarrah (ongoing beyond the Fellowship)
  • Claudia Lavista created performance projects at c.a.t.a.m.o.n and a documentary video at multiple Jerusalem locations
  • Anna Lublina explored synchronization through weaving with Bloomfield Science Museum

What Makes This Work:

Host organizations are not gatekeepers or taskmasters. They are collaborators who genuinely want to work with the artist and genuinely have resources and access that the artist needs to make work. If host organizations see the Fellowship as a burden or a box they must tick, the program collapses.

Evaluation Finding: “Each artist spent time at a specific location in Jerusalem, where they showed and displayed their work. Throughout their stay in Jerusalem, the four artists have been living together in an apartment in Musrara and have received an in-depth introduction to Jerusalem’s cultural landscape while building their specific projects and touring the city. On their own they met many remarkable Jerusalemites who connected them with people and parts of the city that JIF staff could not have reached directly.”

Documentation and Evaluation

The program includes embedded evaluation and documentation.

Documentation:

  • Video and photo documentation throughout
  • Salon conversations recorded (with consent; see permissions section)
  • Selected archival materials (emails, planning documents, photos) collected for program records

Evaluation:

  • Independent evaluator engaged at beginning of residency
  • Regular check-ins with Fellows, host organizations, and participants
  • Final interview with evaluator during Fellows’ last week
  • Written evaluation report delivered within 6-8 weeks of program end

2022 Evaluation:

On Track Consulting evaluation (June 2022, 52 pages) assessed program impact, operational effectiveness, and replicability. Key findings flagged the program as successful despite compressed timelines and recommended specific tweaks for future cycles.

Staffing Model: Core Roles and Responsibilities

The program requires five core roles during the residency period (10-12 weeks). This staffing model is a framework to adapt by city and organizational maturity; it is not a fixed template.

1. Executive Director / Program Director

Responsibilities: Strategic direction, advisory committee liaison, major donor relationship management, problem-solving, host organization relationships, overall vision and troubleshooting

Time commitment: Full-time

Key Learning from 2022: This is the “Elise role.” It can be split between founder/advisor role (part-time) and hired director (full-time) depending on organizational maturity. The role requires deep political and cultural knowledge of the local context.

2. Program Director

Responsibilities: Daily on-ground operations, host organization liaison, problem-solving, decision-making, troubleshooting

Requires: Deep local knowledge and credibility

Time commitment: Full-time during residency; hire 12-16 weeks before cohort arrival

Key Learning from 2022: This role was significantly undersourced given compressed hiring timeline and breadth of responsibilities. The Program Director is the person on the ground every day. This cannot be part-time work.

3. Communications Manager / PR / Salon Coordinator

Responsibilities: Salon coordination and curation, guest invitation management, translation, social media, press relations, documentary coordination, calendar management

Requirements: Bilingual minimum (Hebrew + English); Arabic essential for East Jerusalem work; demonstrated cultural credibility

Time commitment: Full-time (3 hours per salon × 3 salons per week = 9 hours in salon work alone; plus 15-20 hours weekly in curation, translation prep, social media, press)

Key Learning from 2022: This role is an entire job and cannot be combined with other responsibilities. The 2022 program relied on Nermeen Shweiki to serve simultaneously as cultural bridge, translator, problem-solver, and facilitator. The role was undersourced.

4. Finance / Bookkeeper

Responsibilities: Budget management, receipt coding and reconciliation, currency transfers (critical in any international context), grant reporting

Requirements: Familiarity with local tax and accounting practices, nonprofit accounting, multi-currency transactions

Time commitment: 0.5 FTE (part-time) or shared with other programs

Key Learning from 2022: The 2022 program was undersourced in this role. Operational feedback noted that “financial management during the residency” needed improvement. A dedicated finance person allows the Program Director to focus on programming, not expense reconciliation.

5. Host Organization Coordinators (one per host organization)

Responsibilities: Daily logistical support to Fellows (translation, problem-solving, accompaniment), liaison between host organization and JIF program team

Requirements: Basic English or program language, cultural knowledge, problem-solving skills, availability during program hours

Time commitment: Part-time or job-shared with host organization (typically 20-30 hours weekly during residency)

Key Learning from 2022: Do not ask the Program Director or host organization director to absorb this role. Each host organization needs a dedicated point person for the Fellow.

Additional Support Roles (as needed):

  • Professional guides (Disorientation week): 2-3 guides for 1 week, with demonstrated knowledge of local history, politics, and cultural credibility
  • Documentary filmmaker / videographer: Embedded during residency
  • Photographer: Documentation during residency
  • Publicist / press coordinator: Local language press outreach
  • Translator (if needed beyond Communications Manager capacity): Event-specific or ongoing

Hiring Timeline:

  • 12-16 weeks before cohort: Program Director hired
  • 12-16 weeks before cohort: Finance person hired or consultant contracted
  • 8-12 weeks before cohort: Communications Manager hired
  • 4-6 weeks before cohort: Host Organization Coordinators hired
  • 2-4 weeks before cohort: Guides, documentary team, other support contracted

Staffing Recommendations from 2022 Evaluation:

“Staff will optimally include: (i) a knowledgeable, even-handed facilitator (tour operator) for discussions re religion and politics. (ii) a business manager or organization responsible for logistics and financial management—budgets, cash flow, expenses, disbursement of petty cash, material resources and human resources. (iii) a facilitator to meet with them once every two weeks to help them process their experiences.”

The program operates on a per-cohort basis. Cost models vary significantly by city, cohort size, and local cost of living. The 2022 program cost structure is documented below as a reference point; salary ranges, housing costs, and stipends will change by location and year.

Critical Note on Cost Variation by City

This Jerusalem 2022 model is a reference only. Each replication city requires recalibration:

Low Cost-of-Living Example (Kyiv baseline):

  • Per-Fellow total: $6,710
  • Significantly lower housing ($1,512 for 12 weeks) and meals ($1,008 for 10 weeks)
  • Airfare similar; ground transport lower

Medium Cost-of-Living Example (Krakow baseline):

  • Per-Fellow total: $9,912
  • Housing: $2,940; Meals: $1,512
  • Mid-range between Kyiv and Chicago

High Cost-of-Living Example (Chicago baseline):

  • Per-Fellow total: $16,032
  • Housing: $4,320 (12 weeks); Meals: $4,112
  • Airfare lower than international residencies; catering higher

For your replication city: Use local cost-of-living indices. Key variables are housing (typically the largest single cost), meals, and local transportation. Ensure artists do not experience “sticker shock” and are adequately compensated for 10 weeks of forgone income.

Evaluation Finding on Artist Stipends

The 2022 evaluation noted: “Per diem was insufficient for Jerusalem’s cost of living. Artists experienced sticker shock. Artists felt strongly about need for honorarium/stipend to compensate for forgone income during residency.”

Recommendation: Budget conservatively and increase rather than cut. Artists making $40,000-$80,000 annually in their home countries are sacrificing significant income. The residency stipend should reflect this opportunity cost.

Funding Strategy and Diversification

The program requires diversified funding to be sustainable. Concentration in a single funder creates vulnerability.

Recommended Funding Mix

Major Foundation Grants (Russell Berrie Foundation as model): 30-40% of budget

  • Example: $50,000 for $150,000 program
  • Typically multi-year commitments with reporting requirements
  • Cultivation timeline: 18-24 months

Mid-Level Foundation Grants (Bloomberg, Jerusalem Foundation, city-specific equivalents): 20-30% of budget

  • Example: $30,000-$45,000 for $150,000 program
  • Often one-year grants with specific programmatic requirements
  • Cultivation timeline: 12-18 months

Individual Sponsorships (Fellow-based, general arts support): 20-30% of budget

  • Example: $30,000-$45,000 for $150,000 program
  • Can be tied to specific Fellow or general program support
  • Cultivation timeline: Ongoing, 6-12 months for major donors

In-Kind Support (host organizations, translators, documentarians, space): 10-15% of budget

  • Example: $15,000-$22,500 in-kind value for $150,000 program
  • Leverages local institutional relationships
  • Not cash, but reduces actual cash requirements

  • “Per diem was insufficient for Jerusalem’s cost of living. Artists experienced sticker shock.”
  • “Artists felt strongly about need for honorarium/stipend to compensate for forgone income.”
  • “Hosts and artists both wanted clearer budget guidelines and expense documentation protocols.”

Recommendation from evaluator: “A more structured budget for artists and host organizations to help prevent financial misunderstandings. Increased funding for the host organizations based on project need. Increased per diem for the artists, to an amount commensurate with the cost of living in [your replication city].”

Contingency Planning and Budget Reserves

Contingency Reserve Allocation

Maintain a contingency reserve of 10% of annual budget for unexpected costs. This reserve covers:

  • Emergency housing changes or relocations
  • Unexpected insurance or medical costs
  • Host organization emergency needs
  • Currency fluctuations (in international contexts)
  • Mid-program staffing changes or contingencies
  • Equipment or technology failures
  • Political or security situation adjustments

2022 Example: Contingency funds were used when Claudia Lavista needed insurance to film at Ein Prat nature preserve, enabling that project’s completion.

Budget Flexibility Framework

Build into every budget 3-week flexibility in program dates. If political situation becomes unsafe in October, the program can delay to mid-October or January. If host organization has emergency, the program can pause and resume with minimal disruption.

Scaling Down: What is Essential, What Can Be Cut

If funding falls short and you cannot meet the full budget, this is the priority order (do not cut below this line):

  1. Salaries (you cannot operate without staff on the ground; this is non-negotiable)
  2. Housing and airfare (Fellows cannot participate without basic support)
  3. Host organization stipends (reducing these breaks partnerships)
  4. Salon program (this is the core methodology; reduce from 36 to 24 salons, not zero)

Acceptable cuts if necessary:

  • Documentation scope (reduce to essential video/photo, no embedded videographer)
  • US administrative (reduce founder/advisor time allocation)
  • Contingency reserve (cut to 5% instead of 10%)

Never cut:

  • Program Director or Communications Manager positions
  • Disorientation week (essential to methodology)
  • Host organization partnerships

If the program cannot cover all core costs, better to run at smaller scale (4 Fellows instead of 5-6) than to run a full cohort with inadequate support.

Financial Reporting and Accountability

Documentation Required During Residency

  • Weekly budget tracking (actual spend vs. budget)
  • Receipt reconciliation (all expenses documented with receipts and coding)
  • Currency transfer documentation (for international programs)
  • Payroll and staff time tracking
  • Salon and event cost tracking

Documentation Required Post-Residency

  • Final P&L (revenue and expenses with full reconciliation)
  • Financial report to each major funder (per grant requirements, typically within 60 days of program end)
  • Independent evaluation of program impact (6-8 weeks after program end)
  • Archival of financial records (receipts, invoices, communications) for 3+ years

Next Director Onboarding

The incoming director should receive:

  • Complete 2022 P&L with actual figures (not reconstruction)
  • Donor list with 2022 contribution amounts
  • Staff salary documentation and justification
  • Three-year funding forecast (2024, 2025, 2026)
  • Fundraising strategy and prospect list
  • Timeline for grant applications (12-24 months in advance)

Conflict Resolution and Decision-Making Authority

FRAMEWORK TO DEVELOP

The 2022 program did not encounter major conflicts, but the evaluation notes that clear decision-making protocols and conflict resolution pathways need to be established before the program begins.

Questions to Resolve:

  • Who has final decision-making authority in different scenarios? (Program Director? Advisory Committee? Host Organization Director? Executive Director consulting from abroad?)
  • What is the escalation path when a Fellow and host organization have a disagreement about project scope, timeline, or resources?
  • What happens if a salon becomes contentious or politically charged in ways that feel unsafe to participants?
  • What if a host organization wants to withdraw mid-program?
  • What if a Fellow’s work creates controversy or political backlash?
  • How are disputes between host organizations resolved?
  • What role does the advisory committee play in real-time decision-making vs. retrospective evaluation?
  • What disputes or decisions require consensus vs. allowing one party to decide unilaterally?

2022 Evidence:

The program ran smoothly in part because Elise maintained strong relationships with all parties and could navigate tensions informally. In a replication city, this informal relational infrastructure may not exist. Explicit protocols are needed.

Recommendation:

Develop a decision matrix before the program begins, with clear authority boundaries for the Program Director, Communications Manager, host organization directors, and advisory committee. Distinguish between decisions that can be made in real-time (e.g., schedule adjustments) and decisions requiring consultation (e.g., project scope changes, resource allocation).

Alternative Scenarios and Contingency Planning

The 2022 program ran during a relatively stable political moment by Jerusalem standards. Planning must account for scenarios that disrupt normal operations.

Identified Risks:

  • Political escalation (October 7, 2023 resulted in program suspension; this risk is city-dependent but endemic to any location with geopolitical tension)
  • Weather events (2022 notes: “coldest March on record” created unexpected logistics challenges)
  • Health incidents among Fellows or staff
  • Housing disruption (security situation, natural disaster, landlord issues)
  • Fellow or staff mental health crisis
  • Host organization leadership change or organizational crisis
  • Host organization withdrawal from program
  • Communications breakdown (translation failures, misunderstandings between parties)
  • Financial shortfall or major funder withdrawal
  • Media or political controversy
  • Public safety incident

Contingency Planning Framework:

  • Establish contingency reserve of 10% of annual budget for unexpected costs
  • Build in 3-week flexibility in program dates (if October start is unsafe, program can delay to mid-October or January)
  • Establish clear communication protocols with host organizations about pause/resume decisions; document these in advance
  • Have alternative housing options identified and pre-negotiated in advance
  • Have relationships with medical providers, mental health counselors, and crisis support established before program begins
  • Have conflict mediation resources available (neutral third party mediator)
  • Document host organization exit clauses in legal agreements (what happens if they withdraw, and how quickly)
  • Have backup fundraising targets if major funder withdraws mid-program
  • Establish media response protocols and messaging if program becomes controversial
  • Have security protocols and risk assessment procedures in place

FRAMEWORK TO DEVELOP

The 2022 program used contingency funds when Claudia needed insurance to film at Ein Prat nature preserve, enabling that project’s completion. Clear protocols for contingency fund use should be established in advance.

Questions to resolve:

  • What decisions can the Program Director make unilaterally (e.g., use contingency funds for immediate safety)? What decisions require consultation with the advisory committee or executive director?
  • How do you communicate program pause/cancellation to Fellows, host organizations, and funders?
  • What is the communication protocol with Fellows’ home countries/families in case of security incident?
  • How quickly can the program pivot to online or hybrid format if in-person program becomes unsafe?

Post-Residency and Transition

The program’s impact extends beyond the 10-week intensive. Managing the transition and supporting ongoing relationships requires intentional design.

What Happens After Week 10?

  • Week 10-11 (overlap period): Fellows remain in city for 1-2 weeks after formal program ends to process, have individual meetings with program team, and solidify relationships with host organizations and other city participants
  • Communications after departure: Establish cadence for check-ins (monthly? quarterly?) and channels (email, video call?) for first 6 months
  • Host organization transition: Host organizations should have clear understanding that the intensive collaboration ends but relationship continues
  • Network transition: Ruth Cummings and local cultural leaders who participated in salons should know how to maintain connection with Fellow if desired
  • Documentation transition: All program materials (photos, recordings, documentation) should be organized and archived within 2 weeks of program end
  • Evaluation transition: Final evaluation report should be completed within 6-8 weeks of program end

Ongoing Relationship Model:

  • Some Fellows will initiate projects that continue beyond the residency (Sofia at Ibdaa, Claudia’s return trip to Mexico)
  • Some host organizations will want to host another Fellow in subsequent years
  • Some Fellows and local participants will maintain personal relationships and creative collaborations
  • The program should document these ongoing connections and track them as part of institutional memory

Questions to Resolve:

  • How does the program stay engaged with Fellows after departure without over-obligating them or expecting continued participation?
  • What is the financial relationship after the residency ends? (Do Fellows continue to receive stipends? Are there contracts for ongoing work?)
  • How do host organizations understand their role after the Fellow leaves? Are they expected to maintain relationships?
  • What happens if a Fellow wants to return to the city independently? Does the program facilitate that, or step back?
  • How are ongoing artistic collaborations documented and tracked?
  • What obligations do Fellows have to participate in future program documentation, fundraising, or advocacy?

2022 Examples of Ongoing Impact:

  • Vibha’s stone installation at Muslala became a lasting installation drawing continued community engagement
  • Sofia established a framework for ongoing artist residencies at Ibdaa School, continuing her work through other artists
  • Claudia built relationships that resulted in reciprocal visits and ongoing artistic collaboration with Israeli dancers
  • Anna’s work at Bloomfield Museum expanded their understanding of interactive art; they plan to host future residencies

Evaluation Finding: “Throughout their stay in Jerusalem, the four artists have been living together in an apartment in Musrara and have received an in-depth introduction to Jerusalem’s cultural landscape while building their specific projects and touring the city. On their own they met many remarkable Jerusalemites who connected them with people and parts of the city that JIF staff could not have reached directly.”

This last observation suggests that the program’s impact is not contained within the formal 10-week program. It radiates outward through the networks Fellows build. Documenting and supporting those networks is part of the program’s legacy work.
