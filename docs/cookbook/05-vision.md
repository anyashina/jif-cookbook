# Section 5: The Vision

## The Why: Methodology of Serendipity

### What This Program Is Actually For

Bringing together artists, the public, a complicated city, crazy politics, and impossible odds is what makes Elise tick and is the stuff of miracles.

That sentence deserves unpacking because it contains the entire framework.

The Jerusalem International Fellows is not a service program. It's not filling a gap in artist funding (though that happens). It's not providing a cultural exchange (though that happens too). It's an intervention in a particular kind of social and political deadlock.

Jerusalem exists as a city of competing narratives. Different communities walk the same streets and see different histories, different claims, different futures. For the past seventy-plus years, those narratives have been treated as irreconcilable. Either your story is true and the other is false, or they must be kept separate so they never meet.

The JIF program operates on a different hypothesis: what if you brought together artists, cultural leaders, and community members from both sides of the narrative divide and asked them to make something together? Not to resolve the conflict. Not to find common ground through dialogue or education. But to create something new through artistic collaboration at the precise point of tension.

**Vibha Galhotra's work during the 2022 residency illustrates this exactly.** She didn't create a work about water rights in the Jordan River and try to convince people to agree. She created an immersive dinner installation called "Who Owns the Water?" that brought together people from different communities to sit at a table and experience the problem through their senses and through each other's presence. She worked with religious leaders across three traditions to record their teachings about human relationship to nature and built a Jerusalem stone well that amplified those voices into the room. She presented the work at Muslala, a community-based organization on the East Jerusalem side.

What happened in that room was serendipitous, which is Elise's word for it. People who don't normally find themselves in conversation found themselves in conversation. People who experience Jerusalem as divided experienced, for a moment, a different possibility. The work remained on display. It continues to be a place where people meditate and engage in conversation about the foundations of our relationship with nature.

That is what the program is for.

### The Methodology: Five Questions

Over sixteen years, a pattern has emerged. It's not a formula (if it were, the program would not work), but it's a repeatable set of questions that generate the conditions where serendipity becomes possible.

**Question 1: What is the specific unresolved tension in this city?**

Not abstract conflict. Not "the political situation." Not "division." Specific, embodied tension. In Jerusalem, Elise identified the tension around religious identity, resource ownership and control, and the presence of multiple incompatible narratives about the same physical place. In Chicago, the tension might be racial segregation and the question of who has the right to shape neighborhood culture. In Kyiv, the tension might be post-conflict recovery and the question of what kind of nation rebuilds.

You cannot run this program in any city until you can name the specific tension clearly and honestly.

**Question 2: Who are the cultural leaders who have earned trust on both sides of that tension?**

Not neutral people (neutrality is not possible in complicated cities). People who have demonstrated commitment, credibility, and genuine relationships across the divide. People who have skin in the game on multiple sides. People who are known and trusted enough that when they invite someone into a room, people come.

This takes 18-24 months minimum to identify. This is why the program cannot be launched quickly. You are identifying the people who will make the collaboration possible, not the people who will manage the program.

In Jerusalem, Ruth Cummings is one such person: she works in East Jerusalem, she has relationships across communities, she does not accept Israeli funding (which means she has credibility in non-normalization frameworks), and Elise has known her for 12+ years. This is not something you can build quickly.

**Question 3: How do you build the housing, salon, and host organization structures around the tension itself, not despite it?**

This is the structural decision.

Most residency programs try to create a safe container where artists can work. The JIF program does the opposite. It puts the Fellow in housing near the seam. It builds weekly salons where the tension is the thing everyone is actually thinking about. It pairs Fellows with host organizations that are actively engaged in the tension, not organizations that are trying to stay above the fray.

This means the program is harder for Fellows. It means the salons are sometimes uncomfortable. It means the host organizations have to do harder work. But it also means the work that emerges is not theoretical. It's grounded in something real.

**Question 4: How do you select artists who can actually engage with this tension, not just make work about it?**

Not every good artist can do this work. You need artists who have demonstrated the capacity to work collaboratively, who are not precious about their vision, who can engage with communities that may not speak their language or share their cultural context, and who can make work that is not explanatory or didactic.

This is why the selection process takes so long. You are not choosing artists based on their CV or their past work. You are choosing based on their demonstrated capacity to do this particular thing in this particular place.

**Question 5: What does a 10-week timeline look like in your geopolitical moment and season?**

The program runs for 10 weeks. This is not arbitrary. It's long enough for real collaboration to happen (Fellowship programs that run 4-6 weeks tend to produce interesting tourist work). It's short enough that the intensity is sustainable and that the Fellow is not trying to solve the conflict or establish permanent infrastructure.

But where does it fit in your city's calendar? In Jerusalem, the program ran March-May 2022, right after winter. In a climate context like Kyiv or Krakow, the same window might not work. In Chicago, you might choose a different season altogether. The question is: what 10-week window makes sense for your city's weather, school calendar, political calendar, and cultural calendar?

This is not something you can borrow from Jerusalem. Each city has to figure this out independently.

### The Serendipity Framework Is Transferable; The Specifics Are Not

**What transfers:**

- The structure of selecting artists through a warm nominator network (not open calls)
- The principle of intentional disorientation in Week 1
- The use of weekly salons as connective tissue
- The pairing of artists with organizational partners who are engaged in the city's real tensions
- The 10-week duration
- The principle of embedded housing at the point of tension
- The unwillingness to solve the conflict or provide safe refuge from it
- The assumption that structured conditions create the possibility of serendipity

**What does not transfer:**

- The specific organizations (unless they're the same city)
- The specific people (Ruth Cummings is irreplaceable in Jerusalem; the equivalent in Chicago is someone else entirely)
- The specific political tensions (East-West religious identity is specific to Jerusalem)
- The assumption that the same people will be available or interested in a new iteration
- The specific season and climate considerations

Any director who tries to replicate the Jerusalem program by flying to three other cities and hiring the same host organizations will fail. The program works because it is rooted in a specific place and in specific relationships built over decades. Replication means doing the hard work of relationship-building over again in a new city.
