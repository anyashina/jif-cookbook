# Section 1: Timeline

## Program History, Cohort Changes, Structural Redesigns, Key Partnerships

**Status:** COMPLETE (journalistic voice, comprehensive)

## Overview

This section provides a chronological record of the Jerusalem International Fellows program's evolution from its founding through the 2022 pilot residency. It captures key milestones, structural changes, political shifts, and the decisions that shaped the direction of the work.

---

## Key Milestones

### 2010-2013: American Residencies (Foundation Era)

The program began under the Federation for Jewish Community (FJC) umbrella as the American Academy in Jerusalem (AAJ). Early cohorts brought American artists of various disciplines for 3-week and then 10-week residencies in Jerusalem. The model was adapted from the Bellagio Center and Academy in Rome but designed specifically around the complexities of Jerusalem.

**2011 & 2013 Cohorts:** Artists (theater director, choreographer, land use attorney, filmmaker) worked with Jerusalem-based cultural organizations and explored both narrative and lived experience of the city.

### 2014-2021: Strategic Planning & Hiatus

By 2014, the FJC's operating budget for AAJ had declined from $900K to $100K. The board decided to wind down the foundation-based operation. Elise Bernhardt, the program director at that time, shifted focus to developing a more sustainable model.

**2014: Bellagio Strategic Plan** — A comprehensive 5-year vision was drafted, projecting growth to 2 cohorts annually by 2017, with a budget of approximately $5 million over 5 years. The plan included:
- Expansion from US artists to international fellows
- Development of host organization partnerships (vs. relying on a single institutional home like Mishkenot Sha'ananim)
- Integration of East Jerusalem artists and community
- Multi-year relationship-building with local cultural figures

**2014-2021: Dormancy Period** — The program entered a holding pattern while Elise pursued other work and continued relationship-building. No residencies were conducted during this time, but critical groundwork was laid with Ruth Cummings (Jerusalem Culture Unlimited), host organizations, and cultural leaders.

### 2022: Relaunch (Pilot Residency)

After 8 years of hiatus and intensive relationship-building, the program relaunched in spring 2022 with a 10-week international pilot cohort.

**March 8 - May 16, 2022:** Four artists (Vibha Galhotra/India, Claudia Lavista/Mexico, Anna Lublina/US-Germany, Sofia Borges/Brazil) participated in an intensive residency spanning East and West Jerusalem. Each was matched with a host organization:

- **Claudia Lavista** (choreography) → c.a.t.a.m.o.n Dance Group (Elad Schechter, Director)
- **Sofia Borges** (visual art) → Ibdaa School of the Arts (Melhem Bader, Principal, East Jerusalem)
- **Anna Lublina** (performance) → Bloomfield Science Museum (Maya Halevy, Director)
- **Vibha Galhotra** (conceptual art) → Muslala (Matan Israeli, Director)

**Critical 2022 Constraint:** The program was launched within 6 weeks of receiving final approval, compressed from the intended 12-16 week hiring timeline. This affected staffing, fundraising, and preparation depth.

## Structural Design Decisions

### Host Organization Model

Rather than operating from a single residential campus (like Mishkenot Sha'ananim or Hansen), the program evolved to partner with diverse, independent cultural organizations across East and West Jerusalem. This model:

1. Spread influence across the city's cultural ecosystem
2. Created natural bridges across the political divide
3. Reduced JIF's institutional overhead
4. Required deeper relationship-building with each partner

**Selection criteria for host organizations** (developed over 2014-2022):
- Alignment with Fellow's artistic discipline or project focus
- Legitimacy and trust in their respective communities
- Capacity to host and collaborate (space, staff, audience access)
- Geographic and political diversity (East/West, institutional/independent)

### Disorientation Week

From the beginning, the program intentionally started with a "disorientation" week designed to complicate Fellows' preconceptions of Jerusalem. This 10-day orientation includes:

- Guided tours of East and West Jerusalem, old city, neighborhoods, museums
- Meetings with cultural figures from multiple narratives
- Introduction to the city's political complexity and layers
- Deliberately unresolved tensions (no "solution" presented)

**Philosophy:** Artists work best when they encounter reality rather than expectations. By week's end, Fellows are "confused" in a productive way — ready to work without preconceptions.

### Ten-Week Duration

The program runs 10 weeks (70 days = 12 weeks minus 2 weeks for holidays/travel). This timeframe was chosen because:

1. **Long enough** for real relationship-building and project development
2. **Short enough** that it doesn't displace Fellows' lives entirely
3. **Aligned with Jerusalem school calendar** (university schedules built around Jewish holidays)
4. **Comparable to other residencies** (Bellagio, American Academy in Rome)

### Salon Structure

Weekly salons (typically 3 per week, 36 total) emerged as the core engagement mechanism:

- **Format:** Evening dinners (6-8 PM) with 15-25 local guests (artists, cultural figures, community members, past Fellows)
- **Purpose:** Create intimate spaces for cross-community dialogue; allow Fellow to showcase work in progress
- **Staffing burden:** Full-time communication manager role (flagged in 2022 evaluation as non-negotiable)
- **Host:** Rotated among host organizations or neutral venue (FeelBeit)

### Post-Residency Engagement

Fellows commit to:
- Four public presentations in home country (travel funded by JIF)
- Ongoing relationship with host organizations and collaborators
- Potential return to Jerusalem to continue work

The program aims to support continuation of exchanges incubated during the residency (budget: $10K per project starting in 2016 plan; status in 2022 TBD).

## Key Inflection Points

### 2011-2013: Proof of Concept
The early American artist cohorts demonstrated that the model works: artists can engage deeply with Jerusalem's complexity, host organizations benefit from collaboration, and the city is transformed by intentional creative presence.

### 2014: Strategic Pivot
The decision to internationalize and decentralize (moving from AAJ institutional model to independent host organization model) was made during the 2014 Bellagio planning period. This was a bet that relationship-building with dispersed cultural leaders would be more sustainable than maintaining a single institutional home.

### 2021-2022: Relationship Capital Payoff
After 7-8 years of dormancy and continuous informal engagement, Ruth Cummings, host organization directors, and cultural figures across East and West Jerusalem were ready to say "yes" to a residency. Relationship capital built in earlier years proved durable and genuine.

### 2022: Compressed Timeline Risk
The decision to launch within 6 weeks (vs. planned 12-16 weeks) created operational stress but also revealed that the model is robust enough to survive constraints. The 2022 evaluation notes: "Despite the compressed timeline, the program succeeded because of deep relationships established over 8 years."

## Future Structural Questions

**Scalability:** Can the model work with 6 or 8 Fellows? Yes, if more host organizations are developed (currently 4; would need 6-8 for scaled cohorts).

**Geographic Replication:** Can the model work in other cities? Possibly. The Methodology of Serendipity (Section 5) identifies the transferable elements vs. those specific to Jerusalem's context.

**Funding Sustainability:** The 2022 model operated on compressed funding; long-term sustainability requires diverse revenue streams (Section 3: Financial Operations).

**Staff Scaling:** The role of the Communications Manager became critical in 2022; future scaling requires dedicated coordinators for each host org (Section 3 findings).
